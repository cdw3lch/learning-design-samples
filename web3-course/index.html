About this sample

This is a self-paced course I designed and built for adult beginners with no technical background. It was developed as my M.Ed. capstone through a design-based research study on how to teach complex technical ideas online. Authored in Canvas and exported as an IMS Common Cartridge for LMS import; this is a faithful web version so you can walk it without an LMS.

The five design principles it is built on
  • Begin with a concrete conceptual anchor rather than abstract definitions.
  • Sequence system relationships progressively, one connection at a time.
  • Use progressive visual disclosure instead of dense, all-at-once system diagrams.
  • Prefer targeted application over broad, open-ended reflection.
  • Match assessment design to the intended depth of learning.

Note on evaluation: the study used simulated learner data, because the free education tier of the authoring LMS was withdrawn partway through the project (completed per faculty guidance). The design and build are real; the outcome figures are not presented as real learner results.

Welcome

Welcome & Overview

This short, self-paced module is designed for adult learners who are new to Web3. It introduces foundational blockchain concepts through the lens of digital wallets: their purpose, security considerations, and how you set up and use a wallet to interact with blockchain networks.

By the end, you will be able to

  • Describe what a Web3 wallet is and how it connects users to blockchain networks.
  • Identify key wallet security concepts, including seed phrases and private keys.
  • Recognize common mistakes to avoid when setting up and using a digital wallet.
  • Explain how wallets enable interaction with decentralized applications.

No prior experience with Web3 is required. Estimated time: about 60 minutes.

Foundational Concepts

What Is Blockchain?

A blockchain is a distributed digital ledger that records transactions across a network of computers. Rather than storing information in one central place, blockchain systems distribute records among many participants. That makes a blockchain transparent, traceable, and shared, instead of relying on a single central authority.

A model you can hold onto

Understanding this flow builds the background you need to see why Web3 wallets exist and how they function in decentralized environments.

Core Concept

What Is a Web3 Wallet?

A Web3 wallet is a digital tool that lets you interact with blockchain-based applications and services. Unlike a traditional online account, it is designed to give you direct control over your digital assets and access credentials.

A wallet is used to

  • Store and manage access to digital assets
  • Connect to decentralized applications
  • Sign transactions securely

A wallet does not

  • Hold physical currency
  • Act as a bank account
  • Automatically protect you from mistakes or scams

Because wallets connect you to blockchain networks and decentralized applications, understanding how they work, and how to use them safely, is essential.

How It Works

How Wallets Connect Users to Blockchain Networks

A Web3 wallet acts as the connection point between you and a blockchain network. When you want to interact with a decentralized application, the wallet helps authenticate you and approve transactions using cryptographic credentials, all without functioning like a traditional bank account.

The key relationship

The wallet is not the blockchain itself. It is a tool for accessing and interacting with blockchain-based systems. Many people recognize the word "wallet" but miss this role; seeing it clearly is what builds a durable mental model.

Why It Matters

Why Digital Wallets Matter

Digital wallets are central to how people interact with Web3. Unlike traditional online accounts, they are designed to give individuals direct ownership and control. Wallets matter because they:

  • Let you own and manage access to digital assets.
  • Let you connect directly to decentralized applications.
  • Place responsibility for security and decision-making with you.

Because there is no central authority managing access, understanding how wallets work, and how to use them safely, is an essential first step.

Security

Wallet Security Basics

Security is one of the most important aspects of using a Web3 wallet. Because a wallet provides direct access, you are responsible for protecting your information.

Key concepts

  • Seed phrase: a series of words that grants access to a wallet. Keep it private and stored securely.
  • Private key: a cryptographic key that authorizes actions. Anyone with it can control the wallet.
  • User responsibility: mistakes cannot always be reversed, so caution is essential.

To reduce risk: avoid sharing sensitive information, be cautious with unknown links, and follow recommended security practices when setting up and using a wallet.

Process

Overview of Wallet Setup

Understanding the setup process shows how access, identity, and security work together in Web3. Specific tools differ, but the overall process is similar across most wallet platforms. A general setup includes:

  • Choosing a wallet application or tool
  • Creating a new wallet
  • Generating and securely storing a seed phrase
  • Setting access controls, such as passwords or device security
  • Confirming the wallet setup before use

This module covers the process at a high level. No real funds or live wallet accounts are used in this learning experience.

Caution

Common Wallet Mistakes to Avoid

Many wallet problems come from simple mistakes. Knowing the common risks helps reduce the chance of loss or unauthorized access.

  • Sharing seed phrases or private keys with others
  • Clicking unknown or suspicious links
  • Storing sensitive information in unsecured locations
  • Assuming mistakes can always be reversed

Recognizing these risks reinforces the importance of caution and responsible wallet use.

The Bigger Picture

Decentralized Finance in Context

Decentralized finance (DeFi) refers to blockchain-based financial tools and services that operate without a traditional centralized intermediary such as a bank. DeFi tools may let users exchange assets, lend, borrow, or interact with digital financial systems through decentralized applications.

Web3 wallets play a central role: they let users connect to decentralized applications and authorize actions on blockchain networks. In that sense, wallets are not the final destination of learning, they are one of the main access points into broader blockchain ecosystems.

A wallet is not just a standalone tool. It is part of a system that includes users, blockchain networks, decentralized applications, and the rules that govern digital interactions. Seeing that relationship connects foundational wallet concepts to larger ideas: user responsibility, network validation, digital ownership, and decentralized participation.
